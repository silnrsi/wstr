Nuosu Yi (iii) is a Tibeto-Burman language variety spoken by about 3 million people in southern Sichuan Province, China. The graphemes shown here reflect the Yiwen Guifan Fangʼan (The Scheme for Yi Language Standardization. China State Council, 1980). The syllabary and related graphemes are based on the Shypnra dialect as spoken in Xide County. This scheme writes forty three initial consonants, ten vowels, and four tone categories. The detailed phonemic analysis is described in Yiyu Jianzhi (Chen et.al., 1985) and others.  Not all phonetic variants are listed here.

The system was based on the Hanyu Pinyin romanization for Mandarin Chinese, in that Nuosu Yi sounds are when possible spelled as in Hanyu Pinyin, e.g., ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>b</span>›, ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>p</span>›, ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>m</span>›, ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>f</span>›, and new graphemes are introduced where Mandarin Chinese lacks a distinction found in Nuosu Yi, e.g. ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>bb</span>›, ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>nb</span>›, ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>hm</span>›.

This phonetic transcription has been adapted for cross-language comparison in Scriptsource, while seeking to respect other published descriptions. For instance, vowels ‹<span lang='xxx-Latn'>ie, a, uo, ur, yr</span>› are here coded as RTR (retracted tongue root), as a representation of 紧喉 `tight throat’ given in Chinese sources such as Yiyu Jianzhi (Chen et.al. 1985. Beijing: Nationalities Publishing House. Pages 10-12 ) and Xiandai Yiyu (Ding et.al. 1991. Sichuan Nationalities Publishing House. Page 20).
