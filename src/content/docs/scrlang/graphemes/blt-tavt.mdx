Tai Dam (ISO code blt) is a Tai language of Vietnam. It is primarily spoken in Sơn La province and surrounding areas of Vietnam and Laos. The Tai Viet script (ISO code Tavt) is the traditional script used by the Tai Dam and several closely related languages.

There are a number of variations in the way the language is written. That shown here represents the writing of Baccam Don, from Thuan Chau in Sơn La Province.

My primary sources are:

- Baccam Don, Baccam Faluang, Baccam Hung and Dorothy Fippinger, _Tai Dam-English, English-Tai Dam Vocabulary Book_, 1989.
- Fippinger, Jay and Dorothy Fippinger, “Black Tai Phonemes with Reference to White Tai,” _Anthropological Linguistics_, 12:83-97, March 1970.
- Gedney, William J. “A Comparative Sketch of White, Black, and Red Tai.” _The Social Science Review_, Special Number (1964).

Note that I use the “high” and “low” tone series labels as they are used by the language community, which are reflected in the Unicode character names, and which are the reverse of the way the labels are used in the Tai linguistic community.

#### Vowels

Like all Tai languages, Tai Dam vowels are written above, below, to the left, and to the right, of the initial consonant. A dotted circle is used here to show the position of the vowel relative to the initial consonant. But Tai Dam is visually encoded, so the left-side and right-side vowels are spacing characters, not diacritics, even though they are shown here with the dotted circle.

#### Tone

Tai Dam has six tones: four level tones and two glides. The tone numbering system most often used for the spoken language is that introduced by Gedney 1964, and Fippinger 1970:

- Lower-mid level
- High rising
- Low: level or falling, terminated by a glottal catch
- High level
- Higher-mid level
- Mid falling: abruptly falling from the level of tone 5, terminated by a glottal catch

Only tones 2 and 5 occur on checked syllables (i.e. closed syllables ending with /p/, /t/, /k/, or /ʔ/).

I have chosen to represent the level tones with the tone bars Extra High, High, Low, and Extra Low, respectively (omitting the Mid tone bar).

In the written language, three different tone marking systems have been used for the Tai Viet in different times and places:

- Historically, tones were not marked beyond the use of the high and low series consonants. In this system, tones are underdifferentiated. This system would be used in all documents before 1975, but is less common now.
- Since 1975, one segment of the Tai Dam community has adopted the use of the Lao diacritic tone marks Mai Ek (U+AABF) and Mai Tho (U+AAC1).
- Another segment of the Tai Dam community has created a pair of spacing tone marks, Mai Nueng (U+AAC0) and Mai Song (U+AAC2).


Tone systems 2 and 3 are parallel to each other, with Mai Ek being equivalent to Mai Nueng, and Mai Tho equivalent to Mai Song.

Tone system 1 can be derived from either of the other two by omitting the Mai Ek and Mai Tho, or Mai Nueng and Mai Song.

I have chosen to describe systems 2 and 3 here.

_Abbreviations:_ In the tone-related graphemes, -Low C- indicates the use of a Low Series Consonant for the intial consonant; -High C- indicates the use of a high series consonant in the initial position.
