The Thaana alphabet is unique to the Dhivehi language. The script contains two types of basic signs. The consonant signs are considered the actual letters of the alphabet, and they include a null consonant, ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>އ</span>› (“alifu”). The consonant letters form the base line of writing, while the vowels signs—and the null vowel ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>ް</span>› (“sukun”)--are diacritics written either above or below the consonant line, depending on the vowel. The writing of vowels is obligatory in Dhivehi.

In addition to the standard Thaana alphabet, there is a set of extra letters, known as “thiki jehi Thaana” (“dotted Thaana”). These letters, which are formed from the basic Thaana letters by the addition of one or three dots above or below a Thaana letter, were designed to transcribe the Perso-Arabic script. The presence of such a dotted letter is a sure sign of a word of foreign origin. Many such words, if they are establised loans, have alternate nativized spellings without dotted letters. Note, however, that the ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>ޕ</span>› (“paviyani”) has a dot but is part of the basic Thaana script, not the dotted series. Both the basic Thaana and the dotted Thaana letters are included in the table.

The Thaana script accurately represents Dhivehi phonemes and morphophonology, but it has a number of systematic usages that are unusual, and which a phoneme-grapheme table obscures somewhat. One such usage is that of a gemination marker, ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>އް</span>› (“alifu-sukun”), which geminates a following consonant (the alifu-sukun also writes a coda /k/, but if there is a following consonant, a /k/ in coda position will be pronounced as gemination of that consonant anyway). Because gemination is not considered a phoneme, the geminating effect of the ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>އް</span>› is repeated many times in the accompanying table, with each geminatable consonant phoneme. Similarly, ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>ން</span>›, writes any coda nasal, which will be pronounced as homorganic with a following consonant or as [ŋ] in the absence of a consonant. In the latter case, it may be underlyingly either /m/ or /n/. A ‹<span lang='xxx-Thaa' dir='rtl'>ނ</span>› without a vowel sign or sukun (null-vowel sign) combines with a following consonant letter to form a digraph representing a prensalized stop.

Vowels in Dhivehi may be either long or short. In general, the long vowels are doubled versions of the short vowels, but the doubled sign forms a single sign, not a digraph, in that it takes up the space of only a single vowel sign. The second part of a diphthong is written on a null consonant (“alifu”) sign.

The first 9 letters of the Thaana alphabet (ie the list of consonant letters) are derived from the Arabic numerals 1 through 9, while the vowel signs for /a/, /i/, and /u/, as well as the null-vowel “sukun”, are derived from the optional vowel diacritics of the Arabic script. The names of many of the Thaana consonant letters are taken from the names of the corresponding letters of the Arabic script. Other consonant names end in -viyani.

The examples listed in the table are taken from standard written Dhivehi, which is based on the northern dialect, particularly that of Malé. Certain systematic differences in other dialects are noted, however, and flagged in the notes.
