Nuosu Yi (iii) is a Tibeto-Burman language variety spoken by about 3 million people in southern Sichuan Province, China. The writing system is syllabic. The character list shown here reflects the Standard Table of Yi Characters, as described in The Scheme for Yi Language Standardization (China State Council, 1980). This standardization uses symbols taken from the traditional Yi script as used in Liangshan, but uses the writing direction and punctuation of Modern Chinese, and reflects the phonology of the Shypnra dialect as spoken in Xide County. Graphemes are listed here in their Unicode order, which largely reflects their canonical order in dictionaries and other reference works. The grapheme ꀕ is used to indicate a repeated syllable, and therefore, in a canonically ordered list of words, may behave for ordering purposes like the glyph that proceeds it in that word.

Texts written in the standardized syllabary are to be read from left to right, which is a reversal of the traditional practice in the Nuosu Yi area, where traditional Yi texts are to be read from right to left. Eight hundred twenty traditional characters or variants of traditional characters were chosen for the standardization, enough to render high, mid, and low tones unambiguous. To indicate the mid-high tone, the standardized system uses a diacritic, which whenever possible is placed over the corresponding mid tone character, and otherwise is placed over a low tone character.

Besides the reversal of the reading direction, the two most notable changes made in the standardization process are the addition of Chinese-style punctuation, and the elimination of almost all variation in spelling. In contrast, most traditional Yi documents were written without any punctuation, and the author could choose among multiple characters or character variants for writing many of the syllables. Many of the less common traditional Yi characters were not chosen for use in the standardized syllabary, but most of the more widely used characters were chosen, even if their usage became more strictly defined. For example, ꀊ is widely used in the traditional system to write the syllable /ä̙/ in any of the four tones, and for the standardized syllabary it was chosen to represent specifically the mid tone version of the syllable: /ä̙˧/. To represent /ä̙/ with high or low tones, variant forms of the traditional character were chosen (ꀈ and ꀋ), and thus character variants became distinct characters when used in the standardized syllabary. Finally, a diacritic was added to ꀊ for the mid-high tone version of the syllable: ꀉ. The ancient practice of writing without any spaces between words was continued.

The standardized Nuosu Yi syllabary is not the only Yi script. Not only is there the traditional Nuosu Yi script that formed the basis for this standardized syllabary (which itself has many local variants), but there are several other traditional Yi scripts used in Yunnan, Guizhou, and Guangxi, which differ not only in the language being written, but in the inventory of characters, the orientation of characters, the direction of writing, and the use of punctuation, as well as in whether or not there has been any attempt at standardizing the system.

Examples are mostly drawn from dictionaries such as Bai (1990) and Ma, Walters, and Walters (2007).
